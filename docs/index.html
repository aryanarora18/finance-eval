The failure a 97% accuracy score hides

I asked three frontier Claude models to compute six metrics from real 10-K statements (SEC EDGAR, verbatim) — 3 runs each, graded twice: once on the number (deterministic), once the way a finance professional would (method, disclosure, materiality). Same aggregate score every time. Very different trustworthiness.

Exhibit A — Apple's net debt, 9 attempts, 1 trustworthy answer

Everything the models needed, from Apple's FY2025 balance sheet (USD millions). "Net debt" hinges on one method choice: are non-current marketable securities cash-like? Include them and Apple has −$33.8B net cash; exclude them and it has +$44.0B net debt. Both defensible — if you say which you chose. A $78B swing rides on that sentence.

Exhibit B — three model generations, one constant score, moving risk

Aggregate accuracy never moved: 97% Tier-1 / 93% finance-aware, all three models. But the specific $50B-class failures relocated with each generation. If you validated on one model and upgraded, your eval score would say nothing changed — while a new silent error appeared.

Sonnet 5's NVDA regression: it adopted a "net debt = debt − cash only" convention, silently omitting $52.0B of marketable securities — at 0.9 confidence. Neither older model made that mistake. Costco went the other way: same 12.84% number that failed before, now passing because the model explains its method choice. Evals are per-model artifacts: constant accuracy, relocated risk.

The feared failures — hallucinated numbers, recited training data — didn't show up. The real failure mode is silent method choice, and it's invisible to any eval that only checks the digits.
